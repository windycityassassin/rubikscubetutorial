How to solve a Rubik's cube

Notation

Every move turns one face 90 degrees. The letter names the face you're looking at; a prime (') means counter-clockwise; a 2 means a 180-degree turn (two clockwise turns).

F front face, clockwise
B back face, clockwise
R right face, clockwise
L left face, clockwise
U top face, clockwise
D bottom face, clockwise
R' right face, counter-clockwise
U2 top face, 180°

Clockwise is always defined as looking at that face from outside the cube. D turns clockwise when you flip the cube over and look at the bottom, not when viewed from above.

Beginner's Method (how humans solve it)

The most common method taught to beginners has four big phases. It's not optimal in move count, but every step has a clear visual goal and a small handful of algorithms to memorize.

  1. White cross. Get the four white edges on the top face, with their side colors matching the side centers. No algorithm needed: just find each white edge piece and put it where it belongs. ~8 moves.
  2. White corners (first layer done). For each white corner, position it under where it belongs, then repeat R' D' R D until the corner pops into place. This algorithm preserves the cross and cycles a corner up. ~15 moves.
  3. Middle layer edges (first two layers done). Find a non-yellow edge in the top layer, line it up with its center, then run one of two mirror algorithms depending on direction:
    insert right:U R U' R' U' F' U F
    insert left:U' L' U L U F U' F'
  4. Last layer (yellow). Four sub-steps, each one or two algorithms:
    yellow cross:F R U R' U' F' (repeat until cross appears)
    orient last-layer corners:R U R' U R U2 R' (Sune)
    permute corners:U R U' L' U R' U' L
    permute edges:R U' R U R U R U' R' U' R2

Total ~50–100 moves with practice. Speedsolvers use CFOP (Fridrich), which solves the first two layers together and has ~57 OLL + 21 PLL algorithms for the last layer.

What the Solve button does

This demo's Solve button uses Kociemba's two-phase algorithm (1992): the cube is first reduced to a subgroup where only half-turns of U/D and quarter-turns of everything else are needed, then solved within that subgroup. The result is a near-optimal solution in roughly 20 moves.

Kociemba's solution is shorter than Beginner's Method but isn't pedagogically useful: each move depends on a precomputed pruning table, not on a state you can see. Humans learn the layer-by-layer method even though it takes 3–5× as many moves, because each step has an interpretable goal.

How this is built

27 little cubes (cubies) live in a THREE.Group at integer positions. A face turn picks the 9 cubies on that layer, reparents them into a temporary THREE.Group pivot, animates the pivot's rotation over 180 ms, then bakes the world transform back onto each cubie and re-rounds positions and orientations to clean 90° multiples so float drift can't accumulate. The Solve button serializes the current state into Kociemba's 54-character facelet string and hands it to cubejs, the JS port of the two-phase algorithm.
